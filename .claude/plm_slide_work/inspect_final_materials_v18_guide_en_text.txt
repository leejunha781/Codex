Presenter's Facilitation GuideFuture Industrial PLM — AVEVA MarineFor presenting to the Chairman of the AVEVA Marine Center of Excellence, Development and Planning teams, and invited stakeholdersCompanion to deck "Future_Industrial_PLM_Meeting_Deck_EN_V18.pptx" (V18, 42 slides) · Target run-time 90 minutes1. How to use this guideThis guide pairs one-to-one with the slide deck. For each part you get four things: the key message, what to say (a natural talk track — speak it in your own words), who to look at, and how to move on.Do not read it word for word. Internalize the key message for each block, then talk to the room and let the slides support you.Timings are targets. Slide 2 (the run-of-show) is your clock; slide 3 ("How to run the room") is your control panel.Keep one goal in front of you the whole time: secure approval of one bounded next increment. Everything in the deck serves that single decision.2. Meeting snapshotObjective: Align the room on the future direction of AVEVA Marine PLM and approve a bounded, low-risk next increment.The one decision (say it early, repeat it at the end): Approve Phase 2 configuration core + one bounded Continuous Naval Assurance pilot — with named owners, KPI thresholds, assumption boundaries, and the first evidence scenario.Duration: 90 minutes (run-of-show on slide 2). A 60-minute compressed path exists if time is short.Tone: confident, evidence-led, and respectful of existing tools. You are not selling rip-and-replace; you are offering a governed decision layer that makes current investments more valuable.3. Know your audience — and how to win eachThe Chairman, AVEVA Marine CoE — the decision authorityCares about: strategic differentiation, credibility, risk, and whether this strengthens AVEVA's position without betting the business.Win them by: leading with the market gap and the single decision; proving it is already partly built (the executable reference); showing the pilot is bounded and reversible; closing with a clear, confident ask. Give the Chairman a "yes" that looks visionary and safe at the same time.Development teamsCare about: feasibility, interfaces, sequencing, and not being handed a vague PLM rewrite.Win them by: speaking in contracts, adapters, schemas, tests and gates (slides 5, 20-31). Tell them exactly what to build — and, just as important, what NOT to build.Planning teamsCare about: product position, customer value, scope discipline, and measurable acceptance.Win them by: a crisp product story (a control plane above the tools), the 26-week gate model, and the KPI acceptance criteria (slides 6, 32, 33).Other stakeholders (sales, delivery, naval / class)Care about: credibility with customers, respect for certified authority, and a story they can repeat.Win them by: the "federate, don't replace" message and the one-sentence positioning they can carry out of the room.4. Five delivery principles — how to earn the applause1. Open strong and name the decision. The room should know within 90 seconds what they are deciding and why it matters.2. Say the golden rule, then repeat it: "This is not a tool-replacement story. It is a governed, auditable, operations-aware decision loop that makes existing tools more valuable." This one line disarms most objections.3. Lead with proof, not promises. Every claim should point to the executable reference, a KPI, or a bounded pilot. In a technical room, credibility is what earns applause.4. Speak to both halves of the room. After each strategy point, give Development the feasibility hook and Planning the value hook. People applaud when they feel seen.5. Close by inviting the decision, not begging for it. State the ask once, clearly, then stop and let the room decide. Confidence is contagious.Three applause moments — deliver each with a deliberate pause: (a) the "win above tool silos" thesis (slide 11); (b) the reveal that the architecture is already runnable (slide 21); (c) the closing vision line (slide 39).5. The opening — your first 90 secondsStand, smile, slow down. A suggested open:"Thank you. Over the next ninety minutes I will ask you for one decision — not a vision statement, one concrete, bounded decision about the future direction of AVEVA Marine. Here is the short version. Today, no single vendor owns the complete loop from a design change, to naval calculation, to evidence, to approval, to operations feedback. That gap is our opportunity. I will show you that we can own that loop — not by replacing the tools your customers trust, but by governing the decisions between them. And I will show you it is already partly built and runnable. By the end, I will ask you to approve one bounded pilot that proves it."Then move to the agenda (slide 2): "Here is how we will get there."6. Anticipated questions & confident answersChairman — "Aren't we just rebuilding a PLM that Siemens or Dassault already sell?"  "No. We are not competing as another PLM backbone. We are building the open decision-and-evidence layer above the tools they already use — the layer none of them own. That is defensible, and it is lighter to deliver."Chairman — "What is the risk if this fails?"  "Bounded. The pilot is one hull or block, one solver, one closed loop, on infrastructure we already run. Worst case, we have hardened an executable reference and learned something — we have not disrupted a single certified tool."Development — "Is this a huge rewrite?"  "No. It is OpenAPI contracts, a schema, adapters, evidence objects, security and tests. The reference already runs; you are extending it, not starting over."Development — "Where does E3D run — on Linux?"  "No. E3D / Marine / Hull authoring stays on Windows. Linux hosts the control plane only. A Local Agent or plugin bridges them by command and callback. That boundary is explicit on slides 22-23."Naval / Class — "Are you replacing certified solvers with AI?"  "Never. Certified solvers remain the source of record. AI explains and proposes; rules hold the gates; the naval architect and the class authority make the release decision."Planning — "How do we stop scope creep?"  "The 26-week gate model and the KPI thresholds. Nothing is promoted without passing measurable acceptance, and the pilot scope is fixed on slide 24."Anyone — "How confident are the vendor roadmap claims?"  "We treat public signals as directional, not guaranteed. We build adapters and an assurance graph that absorb change without rewriting the platform. Sources are dated on slide 37."8. The close — land itDeliver the closing slowly, with eye contact (slide 39):"Let me close with one sentence. AVEVA Marine should become the open, governed control plane for every shipbuilding state transition — the trusted system for seeing, approving and learning from every major change. It is credible, because the reference already runs. It is different, because no competitor owns the decision-and-evidence layer. And it is safe, because I am asking for one bounded pilot, not a revolution. My ask is simple: approve the Phase 2 configuration core and one Continuous Naval Assurance pilot, agree the owners and the KPI thresholds, and name the first evidence scenario. Thank you."Then stop. Let the silence do the work.After the decision:If APPROVE: confirm the owners, the KPI thresholds, the pilot scenario, and the gate-1 date on the spot.If CONDITIONAL: capture the named backlog to clear before gate 1 / gate 2, and set a date to confirm.If DEFER: capture the specific blockers and exactly what evidence would unblock them, and schedule the revisit.9.
