# ⚓ AI Can Draft the Script — Who Owns the BOM Gate?

Shipyards do not fail on missing ideas. They fail when design-change records, supplier package readiness, and ERP/MES tag completeness diverge at the adoption gate — and nobody owns the proof.

AI can generate PLM data-quality scripts, interface validators, and digital-thread dashboards in hours. That speed is real. But Marine PLM still demands traceability, configuration control, cross-system validation, and accountable technical leadership after the first draft ships.

In practice, the lead developer's job is shifting from typing every line to governing what gets merged: debugging AI output against class rules, defining BOM maturity checkpoints, and ensuring CAD/ERP/MES handover evidence survives audit — not just demo.

## 🔧 Practical application points

1. **BOM completeness gates** — Use Python automation to flag orphan tags, missing supplier attributes, and revision mismatches before yard release; AI drafts the checks, engineering owns the acceptance criteria.
2. **Design-change accountability** — Pair AI-generated impact summaries with human-signed change boards so configuration control stays defensible under class survey.
3. **Integration proof strips** — Build interface readiness validators (document control ↔ ERP ↔ MES) with logged pass/fail evidence; speed from AI, liability from the technical lead.

**Takeaway:** AI accelerates implementation. Marine PLM success still belongs to developers who validate, architect, mentor, and sign off at the shipyard adoption gate.
